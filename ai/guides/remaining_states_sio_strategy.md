# Remaining-States SIO Strategy — possibility-paralysis · identity-transition · momentum-gap

> **Purpose.** The harvesting blueprint for the three states added in the Expanded Six-State Corpus
> Buildout. For each state it defines what the state is (and is not), how to recognize it in user
> language, which insight types and voice registers land, which registers can *harm*, and the target
> profile for ~9 SIOs. Source of truth for the states themselves is `user_problem_model.md §3`; this
> doc operationalizes them for the Discovery Agent. Do not harvest a state until its section here is
> clear.

**Controlled vocabularies (unchanged):**
`insight_type`: reframe | permission | mechanism | story
`voice_register`: direct/challenging | warm/affirming | intellectual/measured | vulnerable/personal | expert/scientific
`intensity`: mild | moderate | intense

**Cross-cutting honesty + quality rules (apply to all three states):** every candidate passes the
anti-generic gate (Instagram test + state-specificity sentence), novelty/dedup, source-integrity
checks, and the magnet gate. Verbatim only from a confirmed transcript; otherwise `quote_type:
paraphrase`/`unknown` and the draft is an honest reconstruction. See
`agentic_sio_discovery_workflow.md §13` for the anti-generic heuristics.

## State A — possibility-paralysis

**User-facing name:** "I have too many real options and I can't choose."

### 1. Plain-English definition
The user has several *genuine* possibilities (a pivot, a business idea, a creative direction, a
move) and is choosing none of them. The blocker is not absence of direction and not resistance to a
known action — it is that choosing forecloses other options and they cannot be sure which is right.
Optionality that once felt like freedom now feels like a cage.

### 2. How users describe it
"I have a list of things I want to pursue and I'm not doing any of them." / "I don't know which idea
is the real one and which are distractions." / "If I wait long enough I'll know which path is right."
/ "Every time I get close to committing, another possibility appears." / "I'm researching instead of
deciding."

### 3. What it is NOT
- Not inaction-loop: that user knows the *one* thing and resists doing it. Here there are *several*
  and the problem is choosing.
- Not direction-collapse: that user has *no* target/options; here there are *too many*.
- Not laziness or low ambition — these users are often energized idea-generators.
- Not a request for a decision framework. Offering a pros/cons matrix misses the point.

### 4. Common hidden beliefs
- "If I choose wrong I'll have wasted my potential."
- "Keeping my options open is the responsible thing to do."
- "There is a correct answer and I just haven't found enough information yet."
- "Committing means killing the other versions of my life."

### 5. Emotional textures
anxiety, crowdedness, low-grade dread of regret, restlessness, self-suspicion ("am I just avoiding?").

### 6. Situational contexts
early-to-mid career with real options; post-achievement that unlocked new paths; creative/curious
high-aspiration personality; fear of regret as the dominant decision driver; "perpetual research" mode.

### 7. Common user questions
"How do I know which one is the real one?" / "What if I commit and it's wrong?" / "Is keeping options
open actually costing me something?" / "How did you finally decide?"

### 8. Disambiguation from existing states
- **vs. inaction-loop** (primary confusion): "If you had to name the *one* thing you most feel you
  should be doing, could you?" Names it → inaction-loop. Lists several / "that's the problem" →
  possibility-paralysis.
- **vs. direction-collapse:** excess vs. absence of options; anxious-and-crowded vs. empty.
- **vs. momentum-gap:** internal choice-anxiety vs. comparison-triggered "feeling behind."

### 9. Likely source/speaker profiles that would land
A person who faced a genuine commitment decision with real cost to foreclosing options and can
describe *what committing actually felt like* — not someone naturally decisive who never felt the
paralysis. Candidates: **Barry Schwartz** (paradox of choice / maximizing vs. satisficing — mechanism),
**Ruth Chang** (hard choices, "we make ourselves" through commitment — reframe), **Dan Martell** (the
cost of optionality, commitment — direct), Tim Ferriss guests on big pivots, Huberman on
decision-making/uncertainty (mechanism).

### 10. Best insight types (ranked)
1. **reframe** (dominant) — commitment *generates* clarity rather than requiring it; "hard choices"
   are where we define who we are.
2. **mechanism** — maximizing vs. satisficing; why more options reduce satisfaction (Schwartz).
3. **story** — someone who committed despite real cost and what it felt like.
4. **permission** — least central, but a "you're allowed to choose without certainty" can land.

### 11. Best voice registers (ranked)
1. **intellectual/measured** (Schwartz/Chang philosophical framing lands here).
2. **direct/challenging** (Dan Martell naming the cost of not choosing).
3. **vulnerable/personal** (a real commitment story).
4. expert/scientific (decision neuroscience) — secondary.

### 12. Registers that may need to be EXCLUDED for some needs
- **warm/affirming** can backfire for the "decision avoidance disguised as research" need — it can
  validate the stalling. Exclude warm/affirming where the user is using comfort to keep deferring.
- Avoid any register that *minimizes* the difficulty ("just pick one, you can't go wrong").

### 13. Example user query patterns
- "I've had three business ideas for two years and I keep researching instead of starting any."
- "How do I choose between two career paths when both could be good?"
- "I'm terrified of committing to the wrong thing and wasting years."
- "I keep my options open but I think it's actually keeping me stuck."

### 14. Target profile for ~9 SIOs
Mechanism (Schwartz maximizing/satisficing) ×1–2; reframe (Chang hard-choices / commitment-creates-
clarity) ×2–3; direct reframe on the cost of optionality (Martell or similar) ×1–2; story of a real
high-cost commitment ×1–2; one permission piece if a strong source exists. Registers: lead
intellectual/measured + direct/challenging, ≥1 vulnerable/personal. Intensity mostly moderate, ≥1
mild. No speaker >2; no platform >3.

## State B — identity-transition

**User-facing name:** "Something changed and I don't recognize myself anymore."

### 1. Plain-English definition
A **discrete external event** — breakup, job loss/exit, move, leaving a community/belief system,
graduation, retirement from a role, a health scare — removed a structure that organized the user's
sense of self. The acute phase has passed; the reorganization hasn't happened. They feel disoriented,
sometimes "smaller," caught between an old self and an unseen new one. The disorientation is the
correct response to a real structural change — but it feels like a malfunction.

### 2. How users describe it
"I went through a big thing and now I don't know who I am on the other side." / "The thing that gave
my life structure is gone and I don't know what replaces it." / "I feel like I've become someone
smaller." / "I'm supposed to be in chapter two but I can't see what it looks like."

### 3. What it is NOT
- Not direction-collapse: that is chronic and gradual with *no* trigger; this has a clear
  before-and-after event.
- Not acute crisis or clinical grief — Silhouette is not a crisis service; route true crisis to the
  safety response.
- Not "what should I do next" — the need is to have the *current* state named, not a rebuilding plan.

### 4. Common hidden beliefs
- "I should be over this / further along by now."
- "Who I was is gone and there's nothing solid underneath."
- "If I can't see chapter two, I'm failing at the transition."
- "I've regressed."

### 5. Emotional textures
disorientation, aftermath/quiet, grief of the old self, in-between-ness, smallness, uncertainty
without acute panic.

### 6. Situational contexts
3–12 months after the event (not immediately); the event can be positive (marriage, big success,
end of a demanding chapter) or negative; athlete after retirement; founder after exit; post-graduation
or post-mission; post-breakup/divorce; major relocation.

### 7. Common user questions
"Is it normal to not know who I am after this?" / "How long does the in-between last?" / "How did you
rebuild after losing the thing that defined you?" / "Why do I feel smaller than before?"

### 8. Disambiguation from existing states
- **vs. direction-collapse** (primary confusion): "Is this something you've felt gradually for a
  while, or did something specific happen that brought it on?" Recent discrete event → identity-
  transition. Chronic/no trigger → direction-collapse.
- **vs. engagement-drought:** structural disruption vs. flatness inside an otherwise stable life.
- **vs. momentum-gap:** inward (who am I now?) vs. outward (why am I behind others?).

### 9. Likely source/speaker profiles that would land
Someone who navigated post-disruption identity reconstruction with *enough time elapsed* to speak to
both sides honestly — not still inside it, not theorizing from never having been disrupted.
Candidates: **Brené Brown** (vulnerability, "the wilderness," midlife unraveling — vulnerable/warm),
**Adam Grant** (rethinking identity — measured), **Rich Roll** (reinvention after addiction/career —
vulnerable/personal/story), athletes after retirement, founders post-exit, authors on reinvention,
School of Greatness / Diary of a CEO reinvention arcs.

### 10. Best insight types (ranked)
1. **story** (dominant) — a credible person who lost an organizing structure and can name the
   in-between from the inside.
2. **permission** — the disorientation is appropriate, not a failure.
3. **reframe** — the old self was organized around something now gone; disorientation = correct
   response to real change, not malfunction.
4. mechanism — least central (some "identity is a set of structures" framing can work).

### 11. Best voice registers (ranked)
1. **vulnerable/personal** (the lived reinvention story).
2. **warm/affirming** (permission to be in the in-between).
3. **intellectual/measured** (the reframe of why it feels this way).
4. expert/scientific — secondary and risky (can read as cold for grief-adjacent material).

### 12. Registers that may need to be EXCLUDED for some needs
- **direct/challenging** is the highest-risk register here — "push through / rebuild now" lands as
  invalidating someone in genuine identity grief. Exclude direct/challenging for the grief-of-old-self
  and immediately-post-event needs.
- Avoid any insight that rushes to "what comes next" before naming the current state.

### 13. Example user query patterns
- "I left the career I'd had for ten years and I don't know who I am without it."
- "After my divorce I feel like a stranger to myself."
- "I retired from competing and the thing that organized my whole life is just… gone."
- "Everyone says this is a fresh start but I just feel lost."

### 14. Target profile for ~9 SIOs
Story ×3–4 (different domains: career exit, relationship, athletic/role retirement, health/recovery);
permission ×2; reframe (disorientation-is-appropriate) ×2; one measured mechanism if a strong source
exists. Registers: lead vulnerable/personal + warm/affirming, ≥1 intellectual/measured; **cap
direct/challenging at 0–1 and only for a post-transition "rebuilding" need, never for fresh grief.**
Intensity mild–moderate (intense rarely; this state needs gentleness). No speaker >2; no platform >3.
**Safety note:** keep clearly within "functional but disoriented"; any candidate edging toward
clinical grief/crisis is rejected for state-fit, not softened.

## State C — momentum-gap

**User-facing name:** "I feel behind; everyone else is going somewhere."

> **Two sub-states.** The User Problem Model defines momentum-gap as the **comparison spike**
> (`comparison-spike`). This phase also harvests the **"lost rhythm / can't restart"** theme
> (`restart-friction`). Both share the felt sense of "not in motion / others are moving," but they
> need different content and have different disambiguation risks. Tag the sub-state in
> `user_problem_match_notes`.

### 1. Plain-English definition
- **comparison-spike:** a specific trigger (a LinkedIn post, an announcement, a reunion) makes the
  user's own progress feel insufficient. They don't want the peer's specific life — they want the
  *feeling* of momentum. Often transient, leaves a residue of vague dissatisfaction.
- **restart-friction:** the user previously had rhythm/movement and lost it (a lapse became a spiral),
  and needs a low-shame way back into motion. The blocker is restart cost, not first-time starting.

### 2. How users describe it
comparison-spike: "Everyone I know seems to have figured something out I haven't." / "I feel like I'm
standing still while everyone else moves." / "I don't want their life, I want that *feeling* of going
somewhere."
restart-friction: "I was doing well and then I stopped and I can't get going again." / "I fell off and
now it feels impossible to restart." / "One missed week turned into three months."

### 3. What it is NOT
- comparison-spike is NOT direction-collapse (chronic, internal, no trigger) — it is triggered and
  outward-facing.
- restart-friction is NOT inaction-loop (never-started, resistance to a known action) — here there was
  prior motion and the task is *re-entry*, often with shame about the lapse.
- Not "get off social media" advice; not "comparison is the thief of joy" moralizing.

### 4. Common hidden beliefs
- "Everyone got a roadmap except me."
- "Falling off proves I was never really disciplined."
- "If I'm not visibly progressing, I'm failing."
- "Restarting means admitting I failed, so it's safer not to try."

### 5. Emotional textures
comparison-spike: envy-adjacent ache, urgency, inadequacy, restlessness.
restart-friction: shame, inertia, self-recrimination, daunting "distance from where I was."

### 6. Situational contexts
visible peer achievement; birthday/year-end/reunion timeline-checks; heavy LinkedIn/social use during a
low period; a broken streak (fitness, writing, study); returning after illness, travel, or a busy season.

### 7. Common user questions
comparison-spike: "Why does it feel like everyone's ahead of me?" / "What is this feeling actually
telling me?"
restart-friction: "How do I start again without feeling like a failure?" / "How do I make restarting
cost less?"

### 8. Disambiguation from existing states
- **comparison-spike vs. direction-collapse:** "Have you felt this for a while, or did something
  recent bring it on?" Recent trigger → momentum-gap. Chronic → direction-collapse.
- **restart-friction vs. inaction-loop** (primary risk): "Were you doing this before and stopped, or
  have you never managed to start?" Had it and lost it → momentum-gap (restart-friction). Never
  started / known action avoided → inaction-loop. **This is the most important disambiguation in the
  whole expansion — see Phase 9 calibration.**
- **vs. engagement-drought:** comparison-spiked / lost-rhythm vs. chronic flatness toward an existing
  target.

### 9. Likely source/speaker profiles that would land
comparison-spike: someone who felt behind their peers and can describe what they *did* with the
feeling (decode it as signal), not "stop comparing." restart-friction: behavior-change and
self-compassion voices on re-entry and reducing restart cost. Candidates: **James Clear** (systems,
"never miss twice," identity-based habits — measured), **BJ Fogg** (tiny habits, make restarting
small — expert/measured), **Huberman** (habit formation / dopamine for restarting routine —
expert/scientific), **Kristin Neff / self-compassion researchers** (shame and restart — warm), Rich
Roll (rebuilding rhythm — vulnerable), coaches on momentum.

### 10. Best insight types (ranked)
- comparison-spike: **reframe** (comparison as signal to decode, not noise) dominant; **story**
  (someone who felt behind and what they decoded) secondary; permission tertiary.
- restart-friction: **mechanism** ("never miss twice," reduce restart cost, tiny re-entry) and
  **permission** (the lapse is not identity) dominant; reframe secondary.

### 11. Best voice registers (ranked)
1. **warm/affirming** (validate the feeling before reframing; lift restart shame).
2. **reframe-friendly intellectual/measured** (Clear/Chang-style framing).
3. **expert/scientific** (Fogg/Huberman habit mechanism for restart-friction).
4. **direct/challenging** — usable for restart-friction "just make it tiny and go" but risky for
   comparison-spike.

### 12. Registers that may need to be EXCLUDED for some needs
- **direct/challenging** is risky for the comparison-spike need — tough-love on top of "I feel behind"
  reads as piling on. Exclude direct/challenging for comparison-spike; allow it sparingly for
  restart-friction.
- Exclude anything that moralizes about comparison ("comparison is the thief of joy") — it fails the
  state's core requirement to validate the feeling first.

### 13. Example user query patterns
- "My friend just raised a seed round and I feel like I've done nothing with my twenties."
- "Everyone at the reunion seemed further along and I can't shake it."
- "I had a great gym routine, fell off for two months, and now restarting feels impossible."
- "How do I get back into writing after I broke my streak and feel like a fraud?"

### 14. Target profile for ~9 SIOs
comparison-spike: reframe (comparison-as-signal) ×2, story (felt-behind and decoded it) ×1–2.
restart-friction: mechanism (never-miss-twice / reduce restart cost / tiny re-entry) ×2, permission
(lapse ≠ identity) ×1–2, reframe ×1. Registers: lead warm/affirming + intellectual/measured + ≥1
expert/scientific; **cap direct/challenging at ≤1 and keep it on restart-friction only.** Intensity
mostly mild–moderate. No speaker >2; no platform >3. Watch source concentration — James Clear / habit
content can dominate restart-friction; force register/speaker diversity.

## Harvesting order recommendation
1. **identity-transition** — richest, most clearly differentiated source material (reinvention
   stories); lowest disambiguation risk if the trigger-event question is applied.
2. **possibility-paralysis** — strong named sources (Schwartz, Chang); main risk is inaction-loop
   confusion, handled by the "name the one thing?" question.
3. **momentum-gap** — hardest: two sub-states, the restart-friction↔inaction-loop confusion, and
   source-concentration risk on habit content. Harvest last and calibrate disambiguation hardest.
