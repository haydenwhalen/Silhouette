The thing that I think most people misunderstand about motivation is that it's not just about the peaks — the moments of excitement, the big wins, the things that used to get you going. It's about where your baseline is. Your dopamine system has two modes: there's a tonic level, which is your baseline — the steady background hum of motivation and drive that makes you want to engage with life. And then there are phasic peaks — the spikes of dopamine that come from achieving something, from novelty, from reward. Here's the problem: every time you experience one of those peaks, your baseline drops a bit below where it was. That's by design. The brain compensates. And if you keep seeking peaks — through caffeine, through achievement, through stimulation of any kind — without allowing intervals where you're just experiencing ordinary life at ordinary levels, the baseline keeps sinking. Over time, you end up in what I'd describe as a chronically depleted state. The things that used to feel engaging just feel flat. That's not a personality issue. That's not evidence you've chosen the wrong career or that you've lost your ambition. That is a dopamine system running below baseline.
