documented argument from her 2009 TED talk "Your elusive creative genius." It is NOT a

Let me tell you about the fear, because I know it well. After a book of mine did unexpectedly, almost frighteningly well, I kept hearing the same question: aren't you afraid you'll never be able to top it? And underneath that question is a brutal idea — that I am a genius, and that the whole thing rests on me. That weight is unbearable. And unbearable weight is exactly what makes people stop showing up for their work. So I went looking for an older idea. The ancient Greeks and Romans did not believe that a person was a genius; they believed that a person had a genius — a separate, semi-divine helper who might or might not turn up on a given day. I find that gentler, and truer to how the work actually feels from the inside. It means your only real job is to show up and do your part, faithfully, whether or not the magic arrives. Whether the genius shows up, whether the result is any good — that was never entirely yours to control, and it was never entirely yours to be crushed by, either. So just show up. Do your job. Keep doing your job.
