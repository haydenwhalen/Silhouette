content_summary: "In his 2005 TED talk, psychologist Barry Schwartz presents the core argument of his book The Paradox of Choice: that as the number of available choices increases beyond a threshold, it stops liberating people and begins to paralyze and depress them. He introduces the maximizer/satisficer distinction — maximizers feel compelled to find the objectively best option and must evaluate all alternatives, which makes every additional choice a heavier psychological burden. Satisficers, by contrast, set a \"good enough\" standard and stop searching once it is met. Schwartz documents that maximizers consistently report lower life satisfaction, less happiness, and more depression than satisficers — even when they objectively make better choices. The talk connects the abstract economics of choice freedom to a felt experience recognizable to possibility-paralysis users: more options does not feel like more freedom; it feels like a heavier cage."
