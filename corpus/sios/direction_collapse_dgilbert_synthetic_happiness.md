We tend to think of happiness as something we find when we finally get what we want. But here is what the research actually shows. We have, inside us, something I'd call a psychological immune system — a set of largely nonconscious processes that help us change our view of the world so that we can feel better about the world we actually find ourselves in. It lets us manufacture happiness. And we assume that this "synthetic" happiness — the kind we make when we don't get what we wanted — must be inferior to the "natural" happiness we feel when everything goes our way. The data say otherwise: synthetic happiness is every bit as real and every bit as enduring. The deeper trouble is that we are terrible forecasters. We wildly overestimate how much any single outcome will change how we feel, and for how long. So when you finally reach the thing you were certain would complete you, and you feel strangely, quietly flat — that is not evidence that you chose the wrong thing. It is evidence that the outcome was always going to matter less than your mind so confidently promised it would.
