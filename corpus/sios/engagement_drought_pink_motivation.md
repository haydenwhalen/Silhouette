There is a mismatch between what science knows and what business does. Here is what science knows. Take a simple task — clear instructions, one obvious path to the answer — and if-then rewards, the carrot of more money for more output, work great. But take a task that requires even rudimentary creativity, where the solution isn't sitting right in front of you, and those very same rewards reliably make performance worse. This isn't a fluke or an aberration. It is one of the most robust findings in social science, and also one of the most ignored. The higher incentives narrowed people's focus at exactly the moment they needed to think more broadly. So if your work has gone flat and gray and dead, the question probably isn't "why can't I just try harder?" Because the real engine of engagement was never the external reward in the first place. It's intrinsic. It's autonomy — the desire to direct our own lives. It's mastery — the urge to get better and better at something that matters. And it's purpose — the sense that we're in service of something larger than ourselves. When the work stops feeding those three, it goes dark, no matter how hard you push on it.
