key_claim: "Excitement about an option hides its risks, which is part of why a big choice can stall — some part of you senses the danger you can't articulate. The pre-mortem flips the frame: imagine it's already a year from now and the decision has failed — a flat-out disaster — and treat that failure as certain. Assuming it failed (rather than asking whether it might) frees you to name the specific reasons you'd otherwise suppress."

content_summary: "Decision researcher Gary Klein, who pioneered the pre-mortem, walks through it: 'I'm looking in an imaginary crystal ball, and it's now six months from now, and this project has gone off the rails in a major way. It's been a disaster... The crystal ball isn't showing us why it failed. It's just showing us that it has failed.' Working backward from a certain failure surfaces concrete risks that ordinary optimism keeps invisible. Klein frames it for a team, but it is a portable tool for any high-stakes personal choice: before committing, assume the path you're leaning toward failed, then ask what went wrong — and let those answers inform the decision."
