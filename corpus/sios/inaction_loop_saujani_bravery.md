Here's what I've watched happen, and what I lived myself. We teach so many of us to be perfect — and then we wonder why, the moment something gets genuinely hard, we'd rather not try at all than try and risk looking foolish. In my coding classes I would watch a student stare at a blank screen, having just deleted the imperfect code she had actually written, so that the teacher would see nothing at all instead of an attempt that wasn't yet right. I understand it completely. For thirty-three years I played it safe. I did the things I was already good at. The first genuinely brave thing I ever did was run for Congress — and lose, badly, in public. And it turned out to be the best thing I ever did, because it taught me that bravery is not a personality you're born with. It's a skill you build by doing imperfect things on purpose. So here is the reframe: perfection is not the goal you keep failing to reach. Perfection is the thing keeping you from ever starting. Do it badly. Do it scared. Do it half-finished, and let people see it before it's ready.
