Here's what's actually happening when you keep not doing the thing. You have the impulse — to make the call, to send the message, to open the document, to start the workout. The impulse arrives. And then there is a window. The window is short — call it five seconds, give or take. In that window, your prefrontal cortex begins to generate the counter-argument. "I'll do it after lunch." "I need to read one more thing first." "This isn't the right time." The counter-argument feels like a reason. It is not a reason. It is your hesitation circuit completing its work. Once the rationalization appears, the impulse is dead. The window has closed. The reason you're not acting is not that you're weak, or undisciplined, or lazy. The reason you're not acting is that you keep losing the race between the impulse and the rationalization. If you move — physically, immediately, within the window — the rationalization doesn't get to complete. The body acts before the mind talks the body out of it. Once you understand that, you understand why willpower has nothing to do with this. It's a timing problem.
