There's a word for the feeling that so many people have been describing this year — a sense of stagnation, a flatness that isn't quite depression, a muted motivational quality that doesn't have anything obviously wrong at its center. The word is languishing. It comes from the sociologist Corey Keyes, who described mental health as a spectrum, not a binary. On one end is flourishing — the experience of vitality, engagement, meaning. On the other end is depression. Languishing sits in the middle: not the absence of well-being, but the absence of presence. You're functional. You're getting things done. The hours pass. But the quality of engagement that used to be there isn't there. The reason naming this matters is that without a word for it, people try to make it fit categories that don't apply. It isn't depression. It isn't burnout. It's the dulling that happens when a person stops moving toward something that pulls them. The naming itself is part of the intervention — once you have language for a state, you stop interpreting it as a personal failure and start treating it as a recognizable condition.
