I learned the value of sleep the hard way. Two and a half years ago, I collapsed from exhaustion and sleep deprivation. I hit my head on my desk on the way down, broke my cheekbone, and woke up in a pool of my own blood. Since then, I've spent a lot of time thinking about how we got here — to a culture where people actually brag about how little they slept, where running on empty has somehow become a badge of how hard we work and how much we care. We treat depletion as if it were a measure of dedication. But here is what I've come to believe: you cannot lead well, or think clearly, or genuinely care about your work, from a place of total empty. That flatness, that going-through-the-motions feeling — sometimes it is not a sign that you need to push harder. Sometimes it is a sign that you are simply depleted. And the most productive, most reconnecting thing you can possibly do may be the very thing our culture mistakes for laziness: to stop, and to actually rest. We are, quite literally, going to sleep our way to a better life.
