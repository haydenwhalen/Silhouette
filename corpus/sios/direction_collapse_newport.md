The advice to "follow your passion" sounds compelling because it has a deep cultural resonance — but the empirical case for it is much weaker than people assume. When you look closely at the small number of people who do report passionate, meaningful work, the pattern is not that they found a pre-existing calling and then pursued it. The pattern is closer to the opposite: they started with something tractable, often something they were merely interested in or even ambivalent about, and they invested years of deliberate effort to become genuinely rare and valuable at it. The passion came after the skill. It emerged from the engagement of doing demanding work well. The reason this matters is that the standard advice — introspect, find what you love, then go do it — sends people to look in a place where the answer usually isn't. The question "what is my passion?" is often unanswerable in the abstract because passion is not a thing waiting to be discovered. It is a quality that develops in relationship with work that you've gotten good at. Stop searching inward. Start building outward. The clarity follows.
